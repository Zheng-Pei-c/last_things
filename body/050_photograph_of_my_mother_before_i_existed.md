# Photograph of My Mother Before I Existed

There is a photograph of a mother on a beach before her daughter existed, and every time the daughter sees it a version of the daughter dies.

It is the part that imagines parents arrive already parent-shaped, with sensible shoes and concern prepared. In the photograph she is laughing at something outside the frame, young enough to trust sunlight with her whole face. She belongs not to the daughter but to weather, friends, music, and an afternoon that owed the daughter nothing.

The daughter grieved this at twenty. By forty she found it liberating. Her mother had a life before that witness, which meant the daughter did not carry sole responsibility for her reality. Love could survive that death of centrality.
