# The Clockmaker and the End of Linear Time

Old Petrov repaired clocks but did not believe them.

"Useful liars," he called them, polishing a brass face until afternoon doubled itself in the glass.

One winter, after his wife died, he stopped setting the clocks to the same hour. In the shop window the carriage clock lingered at 11:04, the wall clock insisted on 3:17, and the tiny travel clock from Odessa remained forever on the minute she had laughed at a customs officer. Customers complained. Petrov shrugged. "Choose your century," he said.

At first people found this intolerable. Then they found it accurate. Widows came in to stand before the clocks. Teenagers came to buy lateness as philosophy. Even the mayor, who privately preferred order to truth, admitted the town had become kinder since not all grief was required to obey official time.

When Petrov died, they restored the clocks for one solemn week. After that, the town let them drift again.
