# How the Train Station Learned to Die

The train station had spent a century rehearsing departure for other people and was therefore unprepared for its own.

First came reduced service, then scaffolding, then the peculiar tenderness of photographers documenting doomed architecture. Commuters hurried through its high echoing hall with the guilt of relatives who visit only when illness becomes visible.

On the final day, trains still came and went. This seemed appropriate. Buildings, like persons, often continue their habits into the edge of disappearance.

After closure the station became a library. Children now run where ticket inspectors once strode. In the reading room, directly beneath the old departures board, people lose track of time in a much quieter fashion.

The station, if consulted, would probably call this an honorable second career.
