# My Father Retires from Anger

My father did not stop being angry all at once. He retired from it the way men of his generation retired from factories: suspiciously, without hobbies, carrying the sound in his bones.

For decades anger had been his fluency. He spoke it at traffic, government, weather, and tenderness. It had protected him from humiliation by arriving before embarrassment could sit down. We learned its weather patterns and set the table accordingly.

Then his heart objected. Not metaphorically. A cardiologist with excellent eyebrows informed him that rage was making unreasonable demands on the machinery.

At first he tried moderation and looked ridiculous in it, like a wolf in reading glasses. But slowly the old trade began to die. He started taking walks after supper. He learned the names of local trees, which is a known gateway habit to serenity. One evening I heard him apologize to a saucepan for blaming it for the soup.

The house changed acoustically. Rooms that had once braced themselves began to echo differently. My mother sang in the kitchen with the radio. I came to visit and forgot to be careful.

When he died years later, we buried many things with him, but not the anger. That had gone ahead, stripped for parts, and returned as gentleness in his grandchildren.
