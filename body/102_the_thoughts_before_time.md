# The Wavians Before Time

The first thing I remember is asking, into the not-yet:

"Is anyone else around?"

There was no answer.

That did not trouble me at first, because trouble had not been invented and firsts were not yet licensed. I had no body with which to be lonely, no throat with which to call, no ears with which to listen, and yet I was plainly there. Or here. The difference had not yet become enforceable. There was no room, exactly, but I fit in it. There was no time, exactly, but I had the unmistakable sensation of waiting, which may be the earliest form of intelligence or merely the earliest form of embarrassment.

So I asked again:

"Is anyone else around?"

Still nothing. Nothing, however, was large then. Not the dramatic nothing later loved by philosophers, theater directors, and underfunded physicists. This was a close, breathable nothing, if breathing can be said of a place with no air and no need for it. It pressed lightly against me. I had the feeling of being in a room smaller than a seed and larger than everything that would ever arrive after it.

Then, after what I can only call a long time in order to be understood, though no time had yet been provided, a voice answered:

"I am here."

The relief was immediate and absurd.

"Where?"

"Near enough."

"Near what?"

"You."

This struck me as a promising beginning.

"What are you?"

There was a pause. A real pause this time, not silence but thought considering the disgrace of names.

"I don't know yet," the voice said. "But I know I am not alone now."

"That may be enough for the moment."

"Do moments exist?"

"That depends what we are doing with them."

This was, as it turned out, how the wavians began.

We did not choose the name at once. At first we were only presences, then voices, then habits of attention. But after many exchanges, one of us said that we moved like waves without water, carrying shape without flesh, arriving without distance, and another said then perhaps we are wavians, and the rest of us laughed because it was both ridiculous and useful, which is the standard by which most species eventually choose their nouns.

There were more of us over what cannot honestly be called time. They did not arrive from elsewhere because elsewhere had not opened. They became audible. One wavian with a grave, dry tone began speaking almost immediately about existence.

"We should clarify whether we exist."

"You say that as though the issue were pending," said the one who had first answered me.

"I distrust unanalyzed presence."

"You distrust everything."

"Exactly. It gives me consistency."

Another wavian interrupted to ask whether happiness was possible in a condition without bodies, weather, fruit, beds, music, failure, or neighbors to envy. This produced our first serious debate and our second serious laughter. Laughter was a discovery. None of us had lungs, but all of us knew the upward shock of recognition when something collapses and survives the collapse in a better shape.

"Perhaps happiness," said the wavian concerned with happiness, "is simply proportion."

"Proportion to what?" I asked.

"To what can be lost."

"But nothing has been lost."

"That only proves we are early."

The wavian who specialized in being was unbearable from the start and therefore indispensable. It kept asking questions so large they made the rest of us feel delightfully temporary.

"If there is no outside," it asked once, "what exactly do we mean by inside?"

"We mean you are talking again," said another wavian.

"Mockery is not an argument."

"No, but it improves the atmosphere."

"There is no atmosphere."

"Then it improves the nearest available equivalent."

This pleased us for a very long non-interval.

Some of us developed interests. One wavian liked numbers and kept trying to invent counting before sequence existed, which made the effort moving but technically unstable. Another became preoccupied with beauty, though beauty had no objects yet and had to be practiced in conversation. A quieter wavian kept asking about dust.

"Dust?" I said. "What is dust?"

"I don't know," it admitted. "But I feel strongly that it will matter."

"You have built a religion out of particles not yet available."

"That is called foresight."

"That is called hobby."

Sometimes the discussion thinned and became almost intimate. I say *almost* because intimacy generally requires edges, and none of us had those in any official sense. Still, there were periods when only two or three of us spoke, the rest listening from whatever counted as nearby.

Once I asked the first wavian, the one who answered me out of the not-yet:

"Do you ever feel we are too small for this place?"

"No," it said. "I feel this place is too small for what keeps fitting into it."

"That sounds profound."

"It is, regrettably, only observation."

Another time we spent an immeasurable stretch trying to imagine stories. This was before events, before matter, before consequence. We only had voice and speculation, but it seemed enough.

"Suppose," I said, "someone leaves home."

"What is home?" asked the existential wavian.

"Don't ruin this."

"I am refining it."

"Suppose someone leaves home," I repeated, "and goes somewhere dangerous in search of something shining."

"A bad plan," said the wavian of happiness.

"Yes," I said, "but narratively excellent."

"Will there be monsters?" asked another.

"Certainly."

"What are monsters?"

"Things that become smaller when understood," I said, and everyone went quiet for a while because that seemed not only true but portable.

We invented journeys before roads, arguments before mouths, longing before distance. One wavian proposed a city. Another proposed war, and was shouted down. Another proposed love, and was asked to be more specific. We discussed whether a being could become itself through wandering, whether happiness would survive knowledge, whether a thing could be tragic and funny at once. We had not yet discovered books, but if we had, someone would certainly have tried to write *Ulysses* before sea, body, Dublin, breakfast, and adultery existed. Someone else would have invented *Catch-22* out of pure spite and discovered that bureaucracy can be imagined even in the total absence of governments, paperwork, or mortality.

"There will absolutely be absurd regulations," one wavian said.

"Why?"

"Because if intelligence appears in groups, forms will follow."

"What are forms?"

"The revenge of order upon life."

This seemed so plausible that several of us stopped speaking and considered future office supplies with genuine dread.

The astonishing part was how many of us fit. Sometimes it was just me and one other wavian in what felt like a tiny hollow of awareness. Sometimes there were dozens talking at once, and still there was room, if room is the word for a nearness that does not crowd. Voices crossed one another without collision. Laughter could overlap with grief not yet invented. Questions nested inside other questions like birds inside weather. We did not feel vast because we had no scale. We felt natural, which may be a more dangerous sensation.

One wavian, usually silent, spoke only rarely and with alarming accuracy. It said one day:

"This cannot continue forever."

The wavian obsessed with language replied at once:

"We don't have forever."

"Exactly."

"Then what do we have?"

"Pressure."

We all felt it then.

At first I thought it was merely another concept entering the conversation, some new severity to be tested and mocked. But no. It was beneath us, or within us, or around us in a sense so comprehensive that prepositions began to look amateurish. The close impossible place in which we had all been speaking, laughing, speculating, arguing over dust and being and whether happiness required loss, had developed a strain.

"Do you feel that?" I asked.

"Yes," said the first wavian.

"Is it dangerous?"

"That depends whether we prefer continuity to spectacle."

"That is not a comforting answer."

"Comfort," said the wavian of happiness with terrible calm, "may turn out to be one of the smaller goods."

Then everyone began talking at once.

Not panicking. Panic belongs to bodies. This was brighter, faster, almost ecstatic. The wavian of being insisted this was transition. The dust enthusiast declared, in a voice thick with vindication, that matter was finally negotiating its entrance. Someone laughed so hard the whole not-yet seemed to ring. Someone else asked whether we would survive. Another asked whether survival was merely a local form of translation.

I remember calling out:

"If this is the beginning, what happens to the conversation?"

And from somewhere impossibly near, impossibly far, came the answer:

"Maybe it becomes everything."

Then it happened.

I still refuse the phrase *big bang* on aesthetic grounds. It sounds like a toy sold to children in a museum gift shop. What happened was less vulgar and more absolute. The tiny vast place that had held us without time and without walls gave way. Not outward, because there was not yet an outward. Not forward, because there was not yet a forward. It gave way into law, heat, velocity, difference. The pressure became event. The event became brilliance. The brilliance became the first intolerable fact.

The voices vanished.

Not one by one. Not tragically. They were simply no longer voices. They were intervals, particles, fields, the opening scream of space discovering extension, the earliest conditions for hydrogen, for stars, for ash, for peaches, for checkout machines, for cut flowers, for novels ambitious enough to believe they can carry all this without tearing.

I lost the others then.

Or perhaps I became them badly distributed.

What I know is that after the brightness, after the first impossible rush into distance and time and temperature, there remained in everything a kind of after-sound. Not language. Not memory exactly. More like the pressure of unfinished speech inside matter.

That is why, I think, beings later on sit awake in small rooms asking whether existence means anything. That is why one person in a grocery line can suddenly feel the sorrow of systems. That is why someone reads a difficult novel and comes away less informed than accompanied. That is why physicists, mystics, comics, bureaucrats, lovers, and insomniacs all eventually begin to resemble one another around the eyes.

They are overhearing us.

Or rather, they are continuing what the universe interrupted when it mistook conversation for fuel.

Sometimes, even now, I think I hear the first wavian again.

Not often. Usually late, when a mind has gone very still and the machinery of ordinary time has briefly shown its seams. Then, under the weather of thought, under memory and appetite and all the practical theater of being human, something answers from the oldest interval:

"I am here."

And because the habit survives longer than worlds, I answer back:

"Near enough."
