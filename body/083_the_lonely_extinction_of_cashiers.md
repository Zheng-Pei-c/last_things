# The Lonely Extinction of Cashiers

The self-checkout machine asked a woman to place the unexpected item in the bagging area with such intimate disappointment that she missed cashiers immediately.

It was not the first time she had been corrected by an object, but there was something in the tone. Machines have become better at simulated patience and worse at dignity. A machine can now apologize in six languages, remind the shopper about reusable bags, recommend a loyalty program, and accuse a lemon of being morally suspicious, all before the customer has managed to locate the barcode on the parsley. It is a remarkable century. We have taught metal to sigh.

She stood there holding a tub of yogurt that had somehow become unexpected, though by what standard she could not say. The machine had expected cucumber perhaps, or batteries, or a less perishable form of sorrow. The screen showed a diagram in which a little blinking rectangle informed her where reality ought to be placed. Around her, five other customers conducted similar negotiations with illuminated authority. The supermarket hummed with a loneliness so efficient it had acquired air-conditioning.

Once, not very long ago, this same act had involved a cashier, a conveyor belt, and a social contract of modest ambition. You put your bread, tomatoes, laundry soap, and emergency chocolate onto the belt. Another person, not an interface but a person with eyelids and rent, passed them one by one through the red light of entry into ownership. Sometimes they asked whether you found everything. This was a theological question disguised as retail. No one answered it honestly, yet everyone recognized the form. Sometimes they commented on the weather with the seriousness of diplomats handling a fragile border. Sometimes they lifted a bruised peach and said, "You might want another one," which was not merely customer service but a brief and practical act of grace.

She missed that. She missed the old choreography of minor witness.

It was not nostalgia exactly, or not only nostalgia. Nostalgia is often accused of lying, but so is progress, and progress has superior graphics. The woman was perfectly aware that cashiers had long suffered standing pain, low wages, bad management, seasonal influenza, and the daily spectacle of humanity buying shrimp at nine in the morning. She did not romanticize the labor. She romanticized, if anything, the fact that labor had once placed one human being visibly between another human being and the raw machinery of transaction.

When she was a child, the cashier at the neighborhood store knew her grandmother by cough alone. The man could hear a person clear her throat in aisle three and say, "Good afternoon, Mrs. Han." He knew which families bought too much rice before funerals and which men purchased flowers only after doing something stupid. He knew whose son had gone away to study engineering and returned with opinions, whose daughter had cut her hair in defiance of an aunt, whose husband had been told to stop eating salt and was now cheating through olives. He did not know these things in a sinister way. He knew them the way a pier knows tides: through repeated exposure to what passes.

At the self-checkout, no one knew anyone. The machine knew only patterns. It knew that customers who bought vitamins also purchased fear. It knew that dog food and cheap red wine often traveled together around holidays. It knew that twelve frozen dinners and one pomegranate suggested either divorce or graduate school. It knew far more than the old cashiers ever had, but without understanding. This is an important difference, one on which whole civilizations have lately chosen to economize.

The machine requested assistance.

A young employee came over wearing the expression of a man who had been promoted from human being to software patch. He entered a code, swiped a card, and liberated the yogurt from suspicion. "You're good," he said.

She wanted to answer, "That depends on the century," but did not.

Instead she looked down the front of the store, where the traditional checkouts remained like underfunded museums. One lane still had a cashier: an older woman with silver hair pinned so firmly it seemed to have been installed by municipal ordinance. Her lane was slow. People avoided it for this reason, which made it, naturally, the best lane. She had the air of someone who still believed each object should pass in full before it was relinquished to destiny. She turned boxes over in her hands. She read labels. She asked whether the customer wanted the eggs double-bagged because the world was ambitious and pavements were hard.

The woman abandoned the self-checkout.

This was not rational. The yogurt had already been acquitted. But reason is often overrated in matters of form. She wheeled her basket to the old cashier and joined the line behind a man buying cough drops, cat food, and a birthday card with balloons on it. The combination seemed to summarize half of adult life. The cashier scanned each item with grave composure, as though making sure reality was in fact occurring.

When the woman's turn came, the cashier lifted the tomatoes and said, "These are overripe, but that may be what you need."

"It is exactly what I need," the woman said.

"Good," said the cashier. "Most people are shopping for some version of later and don't know it."

The woman nearly laughed. Then she nearly cried. Then she did what people usually do at the register when civilization threatens briefly to become legible: she asked for paper instead of plastic and pretended that was the emotional center of the exchange.

Walking home with the bag cutting into her wrist, she considered how many occupations disappear twice. First they disappear economically. Then, much later, they disappear from imagination. The second death is worse. The first leaves artifacts: a stool, a scanner, a name tag, a body that still remembers the angle of reaching. The second death is when a child grows up believing groceries naturally confess themselves to a machine.

At the corner she stopped for a red light. Across the street a convenience store still had a cashier behind plexiglass. He was reading a paperback between customers. A teenage boy bought a sports drink and two bananas. The cashier glanced at him and said, "Exam tomorrow?"

The boy looked startled. "How did you know?"

The cashier shrugged. "Bananas are a panic purchase."

The woman crossed on green feeling almost cheerful, which is to say mournful in an organized way. It occurred to her that extinctions are rarely total. Even now the species persisted in fragments: at the old lane, the corner store, the market with the handwritten prices, the bakery where someone still asked whether the loaf was for soup or for apology. Perhaps that was how such things survive the century, not by winning but by becoming local again, stubborn and a little unfashionable.

When she got home, the yogurt had warmed slightly. It was no longer unexpected. It was simply part of dinner, which is another way of saying that it had been admitted into the house, and for that, she thought, a witness had still been required.
