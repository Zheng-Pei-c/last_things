# Ballad of the Broken Custom

Every winter in the village they carried an empty chair through the streets for the dead who had not yet learned the way home.

No one knew when the custom began. This is how customs become arrogant.

Then one year the youngest mayor in the district declared the chair inefficient, unmodern, and potentially unhygienic. He proposed a sleek digital remembrance platform with multilingual support and seasonal discounts. The elders stared at him with the patience usually reserved for fire.

Still, the chair did not go out that winter.

Snow fell. The dead, if they came, had to stand.

By spring the mayor had lost his election, his confidence, and one glove. More importantly, the village had acquired a silence no app could explain. Doors shut too early. Dreams ended at the threshold. Chickens refused the north lane.

So the next winter the chair returned. It was wobblier than before, carrying on one leg the entire embarrassment of modernity. People touched it as it passed. Children hung bells from its back. Even the former mayor walked beside it, holding a lantern as if apologizing in installments.

That was how the custom died: not by vanishing, but by becoming conscious of itself, which is the older and funnier death. After that, no one pretended the chair was ancient law. It was only a beautiful thing people had decided to keep doing.
