# The Language That Stopped Breathing

The language died first in the market.

Not in the parliament, where speeches are embalmed; not in the universities, where language can be pickled for centuries; but beside the fish and coriander, where old women once argued over prices with grammar sharp enough to skin an eel.

One day a child answered in another tongue, cleaner and more employable. Then another did the same. The old language survived a while in lullabies, in curses, in recipes nobody wrote down because writing them would have seemed rude. It survived in the names of hills and in one insult so perfect that translation could only circle it respectfully.

When the last fluent speaker died, the newspapers printed a solemn piece. But the language had not waited for newspapers. It had already gone underground into accent, gesture, weather. Years later, a granddaughter in another country cut an onion and murmured a word she had never been taught. The onion recognized it immediately.
