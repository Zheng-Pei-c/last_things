# The Night the Poet Retired from Tragedy

At fifty-two, after three divorces and one prize named for a man he despised, the poet retired from tragedy.

He did not renounce sorrow. He merely stopped flattering it.

For years his poems had carried moonlit ruins, dignified rain, and women standing in windows with national significance. Critics admired the workmanship. Readers underlined the despair. Then one evening he watched two old ladies on a bus argue about plums with such merciless comic intelligence that his entire apparatus collapsed.

The next book was full of tomatoes, municipal pigeons, back pain, and the spiritual life of discount stores. It sold poorly and saved him.
