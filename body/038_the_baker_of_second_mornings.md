# The Baker of Second Mornings

When Mara's husband died, the town expected grief to make her smaller.

Instead she began opening the bakery twice.

There was the ordinary dawn shift for workers and stern people. Then, at ten, after the rush, she reopened the shutters and put out another tray of bread under a handwritten sign: **SECOND MORNING**.

This session was for widows, insomniacs, night-shift nurses, dismissed clerks, recently heartbroken students, and anyone whose first version of the day had failed.

At first people came out of curiosity. Then they came because the second morning tasted better. Here was bread for revisions, coffee for those resurrected by nap, pastries for those whose life had already gone off script by 9 a.m.

Years later, long after Mara herself was gone, the town still spoke of first mornings and second mornings as if they were rights guaranteed by weather. That was how grief ended there: not by healing, exactly, but by becoming a schedule generous enough for other people to live inside.
