# Sestina for Objects That Have Seen Too Much
::: poem
The cup remembers lips after the hand is **gone**.  
The chair keeps faith with backs no longer **here**.  
A lamp outlives the argument by the **table**.  
Rain edits names from envelopes on the **step**.  
The key, unemployed, still dreams the old **door**.  
At dawn a child invents another use for **dust**.

The dead are practical. They reappear as **dust**  
on mirrors, on photo frames, where no one is **gone**  
for long enough to cancel every **door**.  
Even absence sits down and says, I am **here**.  
Listen: the house adjusts itself one creaking **step**  
at a time around the patient **table**.

We inherit more than silver. We inherit the **table**  
ringed by glasses, and the diplomatic **dust**  
in corners where the broom refused to **step**.  
Something ends. Something says it is **gone**.  
Then in the kitchen, under poor light, it is **here**  
again, asking softly for the right **door**.

Suppose you open every **door**.  
Suppose the whole bright ordinary **table**  
is crowded with what was and what is **here**.  
Suppose grief is only love wearing **dust**.  
Would you still insist the world is **gone**  
just because it changed its gait, its **step**?

Take one deliberate **step**  
into the room the dead leave half a **door**  
open. Nothing noble. The hour is mostly **gone**.  
There are plums to cut, a bill under the **table**,  
light on the shelf, and in it a little **dust**  
turning gold to prove transformation happens **here**.

So sit. Be mortal. Place your hands **here**.  
Learn the domestic liturgy of **step**,  
of sweeping, stirring, blessing common **dust**.  
By evening someone will knock at the **door**.  
Set another place at the **table**.  
What ended has not entirely **gone**.

Envoi:  
When the hand is **gone**, the house says it is **here**.  
It waits at the **door**, patient as **dust** on the **table**,  
until we take one ordinary **step** and find it less than **gone**.
:::
