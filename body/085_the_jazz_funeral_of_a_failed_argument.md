# The Jazz Funeral of a Failed Argument

Professor Vale's favorite argument died in public during a symposium on permanence.

He had used it for years, polished it, fed it citations, carried it into lecture halls like a trumpet. Then a graduate student with kind eyes and catastrophic evidence asked one quiet question, and the argument collapsed where everyone could see.

To his credit, Vale did not hide the body.

He organized a jazz funeral.

The procession moved from the auditorium to the campus pond. Colleagues marched solemnly while the band played a tune half elegy, half street-party. At the water's edge Vale gave a short speech: "It served me poorly toward the end, but it got me this far. May its weaknesses nourish better minds."

The students loved him after that, which he considered suspicious but pleasant.
