# The Day the Word Forever Quit

The word *forever* resigned after repeated misuse in advertising, adolescence, and imperial planning.

It left its note on the dictionary shelf sometime between *forecastle* and *forgive*, where the lexicographers discovered it on a Wednesday morning and immediately formed a committee, because lexicographers are never happier than when catastrophe arrives in alphabetical order.

The note was brief, bitter, and grammatically immaculate.

> I was hired for eternity and made to endorse yogurt, elections, and men with guitars. This is beneath me.

At first the public assumed this was a hoax. The public assumes this about everything important until it becomes inconvenient. But within hours the effects were measurable. Jewelry stores found their windows strangely mute. Campaign managers stared at speeches in which whole paragraphs had gone soft around the edges. Lovers under twenty-six discovered that half their preferred exaggerations had become unavailable without legal risk. Streaming services, which had been promising permanent access to content they intended to remove on Thursday, entered a period of visible distress.

Scholars of language were delighted in the way only scholars can be delighted by a social emergency. Panels were organized. Radio programs were aired. One semanticist from Utrecht argued that *forever* had not so much resigned as withdrawn labor from an economy of inflation. A philosopher in Buenos Aires called it "the first successful unionization of an adverb." Two theologians denounced this as flippant and then spent six hours privately agreeing with it.

Meanwhile daily life became unexpectedly exact.

Children could no longer say they would hate soup forever. They had to specify the probable term of disgust, which in most cases turned out to be until Thursday. Governments could no longer promise stability forever and had to retreat to formulations such as through the next quarter, weather permitting. Pop songs suffered the most. Songwriters paced small apartments trying to rhyme *approximately six meaningful years* with anything marketable. One man nearly made a career out of *for a historically contingent duration*, but the rhythm defeated him.

The disappearance also had meteorological consequences, or so it was claimed. The sky, relieved of being constantly enlisted as background to false permanence, appeared broader and less contractual. Clouds resumed their proper business of drifting without brand alignment. Sunset improved. This may have been psychological, but psychology is merely weather that learned to wear clothing.

There were, predictably, black markets. In certain neighborhoods a person could still acquire *forever* in whispered arrangements, usually from poets, fraudulent monks, or very old women who had kept one or two in drawers beside pension receipts and wedding gloves. These contraband *forevers* were expensive and often unstable. One dissolved during a seaside proposal and had to be replaced by *as long as decency and health permit*, which turned out to be a stronger vow than either party expected.

The most dramatic effects appeared in parliament.

Politicians are heavy users of temporal fraud. Deprived of *forever*, they had to confront the naked briefness of their own proposals. Bills were introduced promising prosperity for a medium-term interval with review provisions. One minister accidentally admitted that a "permanent solution" would likely last eighteen months and a favorable polling cycle. The chamber fell silent in the manner of a church after a saint has sworn. Several careers ended. A few, astonishingly, began.

Marriages did not end, as pessimists had predicted. Some improved. Without the decorative burden of impossible duration, people were able to speak in the smaller, more exact currencies of devotion. I will stay through your illness. I will answer honestly even when cowardice would be cheaper. I will continue bringing you tea unless contempt or death intervenes. These vows lacked cinematic glow but proved structurally sound. Contractors should study them.

As for adolescents, they adapted with predatory speed. Teenagers are the great emergency linguists of civilization. Within a week they had replaced *forever* with terms more flexible, devastating, and precise. *Astronomically long.* *Until my cells resign.* *For all remaining versions of this semester.* One girl was heard to declare that she would love a particular drummer "past the heat death of current evidence," which is very nearly poetry and should perhaps be encouraged.

Empires suffered most of all. They had long depended on *forever* as scaffolding. Every imperial project eventually reaches the stage where it must call itself eternal because reality has started making skeptical sounds at the back of the room. Without the word, conquest appeared what it always is: expensive, temporary, and vulnerable to weather, boredom, and the local population. Several museums had to relabel their exhibits in a hurry.

Months later, the dictionaries reprinted without *forever*. The gap it left was not blank but instructive, like a tooth missing from the mouth of certainty. People lingered over it. Some felt bereft. Others felt cleaner. Children tracing the page with their fingers asked what had been there before, and the adults, embarrassed by the record, answered: a word we used when we wished to sound larger than our evidence.

No one knows whether *forever* will return. Language is hospitable to prodigals, but not infinitely so. There are rumors of sightings in monasteries, lullabies, and the notebooks of astronomers still attempting to speak responsibly about stars. If the word does come back, one hopes it will negotiate better terms.

Until then, the world continues under revised agreements. It is astonishing how much can survive the loss of a lie, and how much tenderness becomes visible once inflation leaves the sentence.
