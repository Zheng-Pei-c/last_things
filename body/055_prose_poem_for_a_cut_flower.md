# Prose Poem for a Cut Flower

The cut flower is not tragic in the way we say. It is not tragic as an accident is tragic, or as a nation is tragic, or as a train platform at dusk can become tragic if one person says the wrong name and another fails to turn. It is tragic in the older, more disciplined sense: costly, visible, complete in its wastefulness, and somehow more itself at the edge of expenditure than it ever was in the field among practical leaves. A flower cut for the vase enters society the way certain singers enter an aria, already consenting to perish in public if the note requires it.

Observe it on the table. At breakfast it still behaves as if it has a future in botany. Its stem draws from the water with professional seriousness. It keeps up appearances. The petals maintain diplomatic relations with color. Guests remark on it in the usual impoverished language: lovely, fresh, springlike, too beautiful to be real, which is one of the commonest wrong statements made around objects. It is perfectly real. Its reality is the problem.

By afternoon, the first slight alterations begin. A looseness enters the edge of the bloom. Not decay exactly, more like thought. The flower appears to have considered something and found the room inadequate. Light moves over it. Dust, that democratic editor, assembles itself on the shelf and waits. Someone reaches past the vase for salt or a letter or the unpaid bill beneath the fruit bowl. Life, being badly trained in reverence, goes on around the flower with its customary untidiness.

This is why the cut flower belongs to philosophy as much as to decoration. In the garden it participates in system: root pressure, pollinator traffic, weather, the gossip of adjacent stems. On the table it becomes singular. Severed things are often singularized for our benefit. We call this appreciation. It may only be a more elegant form of loneliness. Removed from the plant, the flower is asked to perform essence. It does so magnificently and for a fee it cannot negotiate.

There are people who find this cruelty intolerable and prefer potted forms, sustainable leaves, durable arrangements of branch, stone, or principled neglect. They are not wrong. Ethics has its place in the dining room. And yet one should admit that the cut flower reveals something potted life often conceals: that beauty and expenditure have been in collaboration since the beginning. Not always, not everywhere, but often enough to embarrass every serious economist. A peony opens like a monarchy collapsing in silk. A tulip risks absurdity daily. Lilies die with a Catholic level of fragrance, making a spectacle even of surrender.

Science does not rescue us from this. Science only refines the astonishment. One may describe embolism in the stem, diminished uptake, the hormonal accelerations of senescence, the programmed economies of bloom and wilt. One may discuss volatile compounds, the chemistry by which death enters the air as sweetness. These are excellent descriptions. They are also, in their way, lyric. To know that the flower's smell intensifies because tissues are changing state is not to escape the feeling; it is to discover that feeling has xylem.

By evening the table has changed allegiance. The flower is no longer an ornament but an event. A petal lies nearby, detached with such composure that one almost mistakes it for intention. The vase water clouds faintly. The room begins to divide itself into before and after. Someone passing through remembers another house, another table, another arrangement of light in which flowers stood dying while adults discussed money, hospitals, weather, and whether the train would arrive before dark. Thus the flower leaves botany and enters memory, which is a longer stem than anyone admits.

Children understand this before critics do. A child encountering a cut flower will often touch the fallen petal first, not the bloom. The child is interested in transition, in evidence, in the part that proves an invisible process is underway. Adults prefer the heroic intactness of the head. We are sentimental about surfaces. Perhaps because we know too well that all arrangements are temporary and would rather receive the news in fragrance than in court documents.

Still, the flower is not without humor. There is something almost comic in the extravagance of its timing. Give it water, a narrow cylinder of glass, and a patch of flattering light, and it will undertake one final career in splendour as if saying: since reduction is inevitable, let us at least be formal about it. One might learn from that. Or one might merely change the water and delay the lesson another day, which is also human.

On the second morning, depending on species and weather and the emotional climate of the room, it may have become pathetic. One must allow for this stage. Not everything dying is instantly sublime. Some flowers go cabbagey. Some collapse with bureaucratic inelegance. Roses often remain proud too long and then fail all at once, like certain political systems. Carnations outlast everyone and appear faintly pleased with themselves. Chrysanthemums die as if performing a contractual obligation. Each species keeps its own manners at the edge.

Then comes disposal, the part least discussed in floral aesthetics. Someone carries the bouquet to the bin or the compost with the unease reserved for objects that have lately been symbolic. The stems knock lightly against the pail. The petals bruise. The arrangement becomes refuse, then matter, then eventually the anonymous fertility from which future stems will rise with no memory of this table, this room, this argument, this brief republic of scent and porcelain. We call this ending because our language prefers cliffs to cycles. But the compost, which has no use for drama, calls it continuation in a less flattering dress.

So the cut flower is tragic, yes, but not in the way we usually mean. It is tragic the way a song is tragic when sung well: briefly, extravagantly, fully aware of its own spending, and somehow enlarged by that awareness. It dies into beauty and from beauty into smell and from smell into memory and from memory, if the world remains hospitable, into the black patient work of making other brightness possible.
