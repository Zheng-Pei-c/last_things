# Eulogy for a Cheap Motel

No one expected the motel to deserve tenderness.

Its sign flickered with the perseverance of bad decisions. The hallway smelled of detergent, wet socks, and temporary marriages. Salesmen, runaways, widowers, and women changing cities slept beneath its floral bedspreads and woke with new lies arranged for breakfast.

When the developers arrived, they spoke of upgrades, value, footprint, optimization. They were not wrong. The motel was ugly and drafty and too familiar with despair.

But it had one great virtue: it never asked for a coherent identity. You could arrive unfinished and leave under revision.

After demolition, a luxury building rose in its place, all glass confidence and curated plants. Yet for years taxi drivers still paused there instinctively, as if some part of the city remembered a cheaper kind of mercy.
